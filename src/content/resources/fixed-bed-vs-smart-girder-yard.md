Traditional fixed-bed girder yards are familiar and simple to understand, but they often rely heavily on manual coordination, repeated equipment movement and site-by-site experience.

A smart girder yard does not replace engineering judgment. It reorganizes the production process around a more controlled workflow: mold movement, concrete placing, vibration, curing, demolding and production records.

## When a traditional yard may be enough

A traditional yard may still be suitable when the project has a small girder quantity, loose schedule requirements, abundant land and an experienced local labor team.

For these projects, the key question is not whether the yard is “smart”, but whether the investment can be justified by the production pressure.

## When a smart girder yard becomes relevant

A smart girder yard becomes more relevant when the project faces one or more of the following constraints:

- Tight construction schedule
- Limited yard area
- High labor dependency
- Need for more consistent curing and vibration control
- Multiple girder types or repeated production batches
- Higher requirements for process records and quality traceability

## Data should be project-based

Efficiency, labor reduction and land-saving data should always be explained with project assumptions. A turnover cycle from one case should not be converted into a universal daily output promise.
